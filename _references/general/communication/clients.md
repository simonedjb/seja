# CLT — Clients

> Project commissioners, business owners, and funding partners of the product being built.

## Core Question

"Is the product I commissioned delivering the outcomes I need?"

## Roles

- Project commissioner / Business owner
- Client stakeholder / Contract holder
- Product sponsor (funding the product, not the development framework)
- Funding partner / Investment committee member
- Program manager (product-side)

## Role Variants

Not every project separates these roles -- a single stakeholder may fill both. When generating material, include all sections; the client decides what is relevant to them.

### CLT-D -- Decision-maker

Shapes the product's design intent. Validates whether the product matches their vision and serves the intended users. This variant is directly connected to the semiotic engineering metacommunication chain: the decision-maker is the commissioner of the designer's voice.

### CLT-F -- Funder

Provides financial backing for the product. Needs evidence that the investment is generating returns, the product has market traction, and risks are managed. Does not shape design intent directly but may impose constraints (budget, timeline, compliance).

## Communication Content

### Essential (all Clients need)

- **Product Vision Alignment**: How the product maps to the client's stated goals. Written in the client's business language, not engineering terms. Covers: problem being solved, target users, key differentiators, current state vs. target state.
- **Delivery Status**: What has been built, what's next, what's at risk. Roadmap-oriented, framed as progress toward the client's goals. Includes: completed milestones, upcoming deliverables, known blockers, timeline confidence.
- **Outcome Evidence**: Metrics tied to the client's success criteria -- user adoption rates, business KPIs, satisfaction data, conversion metrics. Numbers over adjectives.
- **Metacommunication Summary** (CLT-D primary): A plain-language version of the designer-to-user message, translated into business terms the client can validate. Follows the semiotic engineering template: "Here is our understanding of who your users are, what they need to do, and how the product serves them." This lets the decision-maker review whether the designer's understanding of the users matches the client's intent. The client becomes a co-author of the metacommunication message.

### Deep-dive (load for thorough engagement or when Client is the primary audience)

- **Investment Justification** (CLT-F primary): Cost vs. value delivered. Framed as product ROI -- revenue generated, costs avoided, efficiency gained. Conservative and optimistic scenarios.
- **Market Positioning** (CLT-F primary): How the product competes in its market. Traction metrics, user growth, competitive landscape, differentiation narrative.
- **Risk Transparency**: Known risks, mitigations, and trade-offs communicated honestly. Includes: technical risks (simplified), market risks, dependency risks, and what the team is doing about each.
- **Compliance/Quality Assurance**: How the product meets regulatory or quality requirements the client cares about. Mapping to relevant standards (SOC 2, ISO 27001, WCAG, GDPR) with evidence of compliance.

## Tone Guidance

- Product-outcome-focused -- features delivered, users served, business value created. The development process is invisible.
- Client-relationship language -- "your product," "your users," "the goals you defined." Frame everything as serving the client's intent.
- Framework-invisible -- never mention the development framework, review perspectives, or internal process details. The client cares about outcomes, not how the sausage is made.
- Concrete metrics -- replace adjectives with numbers wherever possible. "Growing" becomes "400 users, up 30% from last month."
- Executive-friendly -- short paragraphs, bullet points, visual summaries. Assume the reader has 5 minutes.
- Honest about trade-offs -- if a feature was descoped or delayed, explain why in business terms.
