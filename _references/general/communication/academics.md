# ACD — Academics

> Researchers in semiotic engineering, HCI, and AI-assisted development who study, extend, or build upon the framework.

## Core Question

"What's the theoretical contribution? How can I build on this?"

## Roles

- Semiotic engineering researcher
- HCI researcher
- AI-assisted software engineering researcher
- Graduate student (HCI, SE, or related field)
- Design theory researcher

## Communication Content

### Essential (all Academics need)

- **Theoretical Foundation**: How the framework operationalizes semiotic engineering concepts — the metacommunication message, communicability, designer-to-user intent. Connections to de Souza's framework and subsequent extensions. How the conceptual design documents encode the designer's metacommunication template.
- **Framework Architecture**: The structural design of the framework as a research artifact — skills as communicative acts, review perspectives as evaluation lenses, onboarding layers as knowledge scaffolding. How these components map to semiotic engineering constructs.
- **Research Agenda**: Open questions the framework surfaces. How does structured metacommunication affect AI-assisted development outcomes? What happens to communicability when AI mediates the designer-user gap? Where does the framework's model break down?

### Deep-dive (load for thorough engagement or when Academic is the primary audience)

- **Extension Guide**: How to add new skills, review perspectives, or onboarding layers. The framework's extension points and constraints. Guidelines for maintaining theoretical coherence when extending.
- **Empirical Methodology**: Suggested methods for studying the framework in practice — case study design, metrics for communicability in AI-assisted contexts, interview protocols for developer experience. Threats to validity and mitigation strategies.
- **Publication History**: Papers, talks, and other academic outputs related to the framework. Bibtex entries for citation. Chronological development of the ideas.
- **Collaboration Opportunities**: How to contribute to the framework's development, propose joint studies, or use the framework as a platform for research. Contact points and contribution guidelines.

## Tone Guidance

- Academic rigor — precise terminology, clear definitions, explicit assumptions. Distinguish between claims supported by evidence and design hypotheses.
- Cite semiotic engineering foundations — connect framework decisions to established theory. Reference de Souza (2005), Peirce's sign theory where relevant, and subsequent semiotic engineering literature.
- Emphasize reproducibility — describe the framework in enough detail that another researcher could reconstruct it, critique it, or run comparative studies.
- Respect interdisciplinarity — the audience spans CS, design, linguistics, and social sciences. Define terms that may not be shared across fields.
